Workshop 7 - ANOVA: Comparing Multiple Groups

Research Methods and Statistics - T2 2025

Introduction

Welcome to Workshop 7: Analysis of Variance (ANOVA). Building on your t-test knowledge, today we’ll extend our hypothesis testing to compare three or more groups simultaneously.

This session guides you through conducting one-way and repeated measures ANOVA, understanding post-hoc tests, and most importantly, interpreting what these results mean for clinical decision-making in exercise science and pharmacy contexts.

Learning Objectives

  • Conduct and interpret one-way ANOVA for comparing independent groups
  • Apply repeated measures ANOVA for within-participant designs (Needed for Q6 of Research Project)
  • Interpret post-hoc tests to identify specific group differences
  • Communicate ANOVA findings with appropriate holistic clinical context

What is ANOVA good for?

ANOVA is the essential method for testing whether there are differences in the means of a continuous variable across three or more groups, preventing the inflated Type I error that would occur by conducting multiple t-tests. In exercise science, we use it to compare training methods, recovery protocols, or supplement efficacy. In pharmacy, it’s crucial for dose-finding studies, comparing drug formulations, or evaluating treatment protocols across multiple conditions.

Real-world example: When comparing 4 recovery methods (ice bath, compression, active recovery, control), conducting 6 separate t-tests would inflate your Type I error rate to ~26%. ANOVA tests all groups simultaneously, maintaining α = 0.05.

Key Terms Review:

  • F-statistic: Ratio of between-group and within-group variance (F = mean squarebetween / mean squarewithin)
  • Degrees of freedom: df₁ = k-1, df₂ = n-k
  • Post-hoc tests: Follow-up tests to identify which groups differ
  • Effect size (η²): Proportion of variance explained by the factor
Important

Key Insight: The F-test tells us IF groups differ, but post-hoc tests tell us WHICH groups differ. Never run post-hoc tests if the overall F-test is non-significant!

Section D: Post-Hoc Tests with Confidence Intervals

When you run a one-way ANOVA in jamovi and get a significant result (p < 0.05), you know that at least one group differs from the others, but an ANOVA alone doesn’t tell you which specific groups differ. This is where post-hoc tests come in. Think of post-hoc tests as running multiple t-tests between each pair of groups (Ice bath vs Compression, Ice bath vs Active recovery, Compression vs Active recovery), but with an important modification: they adjust for multiple comparisons to control the Type I error rate. Without this correction, running multiple t-tests inflates your chance of finding a “significant” difference by pure chance. If we ran our three t-tests for this scenario at α = 0.05, your actual chance of at least one false positive jumps to about 14%. Post-hoc tests like Tukey’s solve this by making it harder to achieve significance (using stricter critical values), keeping your overall error rate at 5%.

Jamovi Instructions for Post-Hoc Tests

Check the ANOVA p-value, only proceed if your ANOVA p-value < 0.05!

  1. In the same One-Way ANOVA window
  2. Under Post Hoc Tests, move Recovery Method across to the right hand box
  3. Tick Tukey under Correction and Cohen's d under Effect Size
  4. The table will show all pairwise comparisons with mean differences and standard errors
  5. To visualise the group data, under the Estimated Marginal Means section, put Recovery Method in the Term 1 box.
  6. Tick Marginal means plots and Marginal means tables under Output

Understanding Cohen’s d: - Small effect: d ≈ 0.2 (barely noticeable) - Medium effect: d ≈ 0.5 (visible to careful observer) - Large effect: d ≈ 0.8 (obvious to anyone)

Calculating 95% Confidence Intervals for Mean Differences

Since Jamovi doesn’t provide CIs for post-hoc comparisons, you can calculate them manually:

Formula: CI = Mean Difference ± (critical t × SE)

For our study: - df = N - k = 45 - 3 = 42 - Critical t-value (α = 0.05, two-tailed, df = 42) ≈ 2.02 - 95% CI = Mean Diff ± (2.02 × SE)

Interpretation: If the CI includes 0, the groups are not significantly different. If the CI excludes 0, the difference is statistically significant.

If ANOVA is significant (p < 0.05), conduct Tukey post-hoc test:

Section B: Sphericity Check

Understanding Sphericity

Sphericity is an assumption specific to Repeated Measures ANOVA. It means that the variances of the differences between all pairs of conditions are equal.

Simple explanation: Imagine measuring the same people three times. Sphericity assumes that the variability in changes from Condition 1 to Condition 2, the variability in changes from Condition 1 to Condition 3, and the variability in changes from Condition 2 to Condition 3 are all roughly equal.

Why it matters: If sphericity is violated, your F-test becomes too liberal (more Type I errors).

What to do if violated: We will test for it and confirm no violation, but if there was violation there are adjustments for our RM ANOVA that we can make so we can still run our analysis, but we don’t need the details for this course.

Unfortunately, Jamovi doesn’t provide Cohen’s d for RM-ANOVA post-hoc tests, but we can still interpret effect sizes based on the magnitude of mean differences relative to the scale.

Calculating 95% Confidence Intervals for RM-ANOVA

For within-subject comparisons with 25 participants: - df = n - 1 = 24 - Critical t-value (α = 0.05, two-tailed, df = 24) ≈ 2.06 - 95% CI = Mean Diff ± (2.06 × SE)

Remember: The SE in RM-ANOVA is typically smaller than in between-subjects designs because individual differences are controlled!

Summary and Key Takeaways

Based on your analyses, you should now understand:

One-Way ANOVA: How to compare multiple independent groups while still controlling Type I error. The F-test provides an global test, while post-hoc tests identify specific differences between groups/conditions.

Repeated Measures ANOVA: More powerful within-participant designs, controlling for individual differences. Requires checking sphericity assumption.

Post-Hoc Tests (Tukey): Essential for identifying which groups differ after a significant F-test.

Confidence Intervals: Provide a range of plausible values for true differences. CIs that exclude zero indicate significant differences.

Effect Sizes: Provide magnitude of differences beyond significance. Essential for evaluating practical importance.

Clinical Significance: Statistical significance ≠ clinical importance. Always consider minimum clinically important differences, cost-benefit ratios, and real-world feasibility.

Remember: ANOVA tells us IF groups differ. Post-hoc tests tell us WHICH groups differ. Effect sizes tell us HOW MUCH they differ. Clinical judgment tells us IF IT MATTERS.
